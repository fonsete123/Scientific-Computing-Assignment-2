\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{D}{iffusion-limited} aggregation (DLA) is a growth model that describes the formation of fractal structures through the diffusion and deposition of particles. First introduced by Witten and Sander~\cite{witten1981}, DLA has applications in crystal growth, electrodeposition, and dielectric breakdown. The model produces branching structures whose fractal dimension depends on the growth conditions.

In this report we investigate DLA using two approaches: (i)~a continuum method based on iteratively solving the Laplace equation for the nutrient concentration, and (ii)~a Monte Carlo method using individual random walkers. We also study the Gray-Scott reaction-diffusion model, which produces Turing-like spatial patterns through the interplay of diffusion and autocatalytic reactions~\cite{pearson1993}. Section~II describes the numerical methods. Section~III presents results for all exercises. Section~IV discusses the findings.


\section{Methods}

\subsection{SOR Solver for the Laplace Equation}

The steady-state concentration $c(x,y)$ satisfies the Laplace equation $\nabla^2 c = 0$ on an $N \times N$ grid with boundary conditions $c(y=0)=0$, $c(y=N)=1$, and periodic boundaries in $x$. Object sites act as sinks with $c=0$. We discretize with the standard 5-point stencil:
\begin{equation}
c_{i,j} = \frac{1}{4}\left(c_{i+1,j} + c_{i-1,j} + c_{i,j+1} + c_{i,j-1}\right)
\end{equation}
and solve iteratively using Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR):
\begin{equation}
c_{i,j}^{k+1} = \frac{\omega}{4}\left(c_{i+1,j}^{k} + c_{i-1,j}^{k+1} + c_{i,j+1}^{k} + c_{i,j-1}^{k+1}\right) + (1-\omega)\,c_{i,j}^{k}
\end{equation}
where $\omega \in (1,2)$ is the relaxation parameter. Convergence is declared when $\delta \equiv \max|c^{k+1}-c^{k}| < \epsilon = 10^{-5}$. The field is initialized with the analytical linear gradient $c(j) = j/(N-1)$ for optimal warm-starting.

For Exercise~B, we implemented a vectorized red-black (checkerboard) SOR variant. Grid points are partitioned by parity of $(i+j)$: within each color all updates are independent and can be performed simultaneously using NumPy array operations, eliminating Python-level loops entirely.

\subsection{DLA Growth Model (Exercises A--B)}

Growth proceeds iteratively. At each step: (1)~identify growth candidates---free sites with at least one 4-connected neighbor in the cluster; (2)~compute growth probability:
\begin{equation}
p_g(i,j) = \frac{c_{i,j}^{\,\eta}}{\displaystyle\sum_{\text{candidates}} c_{i,j}^{\,\eta}}
\end{equation}
where $\eta$ controls cluster morphology; (3)~select one candidate via weighted random sampling; (4)~add the site to the cluster and set $c=0$; (5)~re-solve the Laplace equation, warm-starting from the previous solution. The seed is a single point at the bottom center.

\subsection{Monte Carlo DLA (Exercises C--D)}

One random walker at a time is released from a random $x$-position on the top boundary. The walker moves randomly (up/down/left/right with equal probability), with periodic BC in $x$. Walkers exiting through top or bottom are removed. When a walker occupies a cell adjacent to the cluster, it sticks with probability $p_s$; if it does not stick, the walk continues but the walker cannot enter cluster sites. For $p_s=1$ this reduces to standard DLA.

\subsection{Gray-Scott Model (Exercise E)}

The Gray-Scott model describes two reacting, diffusing chemicals $U$ and $V$:
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= D_u \nabla^2 u - uv^2 + f(1-u) \label{eq:gs_u}\\
\frac{\partial v}{\partial t} &= D_v \nabla^2 v + uv^2 - (f+k)v \label{eq:gs_v}
\end{align}
where $f$ is the feed rate and $k$ the kill rate. The term $uv^2$ represents the autocatalytic reaction $U + 2V \to 3V$. We use explicit Euler time-stepping with a 5-point stencil Laplacian and periodic BC on all sides, computed via NumPy roll operations. Parameters: $\delta t = 1$, $\delta x = 1$, $D_u = 0.16$, $D_v = 0.08$. Initial conditions: $u=1$ everywhere, $v=0.25$ in a $20\times20$ center square, with uniform random noise of amplitude $0.01$.


\section{Results}

\subsection{Exercise A: DLA with Diffusion Equation (4 pts)}

We performed DLA simulations on a $100\times100$ grid with 200 growth steps for $\eta \in \{0, 0.5, 1, 2, 3\}$ using the vectorized SOR solver with $\omega = 1.8$.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/exercise_a_dla_clusters.png}
\caption{DLA clusters with concentration fields for different $\eta$ values on a $100\times100$ grid after 200 growth steps. The background shows the nutrient concentration (hot colormap: black$\,=\,0$, yellow$\,=\,1$), and the cluster is overlaid in blue. As $\eta$ increases, clusters transition from compact (Eden) to fractal (DLA) to needle-like morphologies.}
\label{fig:dla_clusters}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:dla_clusters} shows the clusters overlaid on their concentration fields. A clear morphological transition is observed. For $\eta=0$ (Eden cluster), growth probability is uniform across all candidates, producing a compact, roughly circular cluster. For $\eta=1$ (standard DLA), the cluster displays characteristic fractal branching: tips that protrude into higher-concentration regions screen the interior from nutrient flux, consistent with the theoretical fractal dimension $D_f \approx 1.71$ for 2D DLA. For $\eta=2$ and $\eta=3$, branches become progressively thinner and more elongated; at $\eta=3$ the cluster grows as a nearly one-dimensional filament directed toward the source, as only the tip with maximal concentration has significant growth probability.

The concentration fields illustrate the screening effect: for high $\eta$, a strong gradient focuses near the leading tip, while for $\eta=0$ the field remains nearly linear.

\textbf{SOR $\omega$ optimization.} We tested $\omega$ from 1.0 to 1.95 on 20 DLA growth steps ($N=50$, $\eta=1$). Total time decreased from 1.23\,s ($\omega=1.0$) to a minimum of 0.43\,s at $\omega \approx 1.90$, then increased sharply to 0.71\,s at $\omega=1.95$ as the method approaches instability. The optimal $\omega$ near 1.85--1.90 is consistent with theoretical expectations for SOR on the Laplace equation.

\caption{Total computation time vs.\ SOR relaxation parameter $\omega$ for 20 DLA growth steps on a $50\times50$ grid. The optimum lies near $\omega \approx 1.90$.}

\subsection{Exercise B: SOR Optimization (1 pt)}

We compared the naive loop-based SOR against the vectorized red-black SOR for 30 DLA growth steps.

\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Grid Size & Naive (s) & Vectorized (s) & Speedup \\
\midrule
$50\times50$   & 10.84 & 0.99 & 11.0$\times$ \\
$100\times100$ & 36.45 & 1.37 & 26.7$\times$ \\
$150\times150$ & ---   & 2.24 & --- \\

The vectorized solver achieves an 11$\times$ speedup at $N\!=\!50$ and 27$\times$ at $N\!=\!100$ (Table~\ref{tab:sor_timing}, Fig.~\ref{fig:optimization}). The speedup grows with grid size because the naive solver's overhead scales as $O(N^2)$ Python-level loop iterations per SOR sweep, while the vectorized solver performs the same updates via NumPy's C-level array operations. For $N\!=\!150$, the naive solver was prohibitively slow ($>120$\,s estimated), while the vectorized solver completed in 2.24\,s, demonstrating the practical necessity of vectorization for larger grids.

\subsection{Exercise C: Monte Carlo DLA (2 pts)}

Fig.~\ref{fig:mc_dla} shows a Monte Carlo DLA cluster grown with 300 particles on a $100\times100$ grid ($p_s\!=\!1$). The cluster displays fractal branching comparable to diffusion-based DLA with $\eta\!=\!1$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:dla_clusters}). This is expected: the random walk of particles produces, in the ensemble average, a concentration field satisfying the Laplace equation, so the probability of a walker reaching a boundary site is proportional to the local concentration---exactly matching $\eta\!=\!1$ growth.

The Monte Carlo cluster shows somewhat more stochastic branching compared to the diffusion result, reflecting the particle-by-particle nature of growth. However, the overall morphology---branching arms, tip-splitting, interior screening---is qualitatively identical. The simulation completed in approximately 66\,s for 300 particles.

\subsection{Exercise D: Sticking Probability (1 pt)}

Fig.~\ref{fig:sticking} shows clusters for $p_s \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 1.0\}$, each with 300 particles. As $p_s$ decreases, the clusters become progressively more compact. At $p_s\!=\!1$, walkers stick on first contact, so protruding tips capture most walkers (screening effect), producing open fractal branches. At $p_s\!=\!0.1$, walkers that fail to stick continue diffusing, penetrating into gaps before eventually attaching. This effectively averages out concentration gradients and reduces the tip effect, producing compact clusters analogous to low-$\eta$ diffusion DLA. In the limit $p_s \to 0$, growth would approach the Eden model.

Computation time increases as $p_s$ decreases (112\,s for $p_s\!=\!0.1$ vs.\ 62\,s for $p_s\!=\!1.0$) because each walker requires more steps before sticking.

\subsection{Exercise E: Gray-Scott Model (3 pts)}

\textbf{Time evolution} ($f\!=\!0.035$, $k\!=\!0.060$). Fig.~\ref{fig:gs_time} shows the concentration of $v$ at six time points on a $256\times256$ grid. The initial square perturbation develops into a four-lobed structure by $t\!=\!1000$. By $t\!=\!2500$, fingering instabilities emerge. At $t\!=\!5000$, a labyrinthine stripe pattern has developed, which fills the domain by $t\!=\!10000$. This is the classic Turing instability: the difference in diffusion rates ($D_u > D_v$) destabilizes the homogeneous state to spatial perturbations of a characteristic wavelength, causing the activator ($v$) to self-organize into periodic structures.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/exercise_e_time_evolution.png}
\caption{Gray-Scott time evolution ($f\!=\!0.035$, $k\!=\!0.060$, $N\!=\!256$). The $v$ concentration is shown at $t = 0, 1000, 2500, 5000, 7500, 10000$. The initial perturbation develops into a labyrinthine Turing pattern. Colorbar: $v$ concentration (inferno colormap).}
\label{fig:gs_time}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Parameter comparison.} Fig.~\ref{fig:gs_params} shows steady-state patterns for four $(f,k)$ combinations. The labyrinth case ($f\!=\!0.035$, $k\!=\!0.060$) produces dense interdigitating stripes. The spots case ($f\!=\!0.030$, $k\!=\!0.057$) yields wider stripes with a larger characteristic wavelength due to the lower feed rate. The stripes case ($f\!=\!0.025$, $k\!=\!0.055$) shows wide, well-separated stripes that propagate slowly. The waves case ($f\!=\!0.040$, $k\!=\!0.060$) produces finer features driven by a stronger instability. These results are consistent with the rich phase diagram documented by Pearson~\cite{pearson1993}, demonstrating how the ratio $f/k$ controls pattern morphology.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

The diffusion-based DLA correctly reproduces the expected morphological transition from compact Eden clusters ($\eta\!=\!0$) through fractal DLA ($\eta\!=\!1$) to needle-like structures ($\eta > 1$). The SOR solver, initialized with the analytical gradient and warm-started between growth steps, converges efficiently with optimal $\omega \approx 1.90$ for the $100\times100$ grid. The vectorized red-black implementation provides up to $27\times$ speedup over naive loops, enabling practical simulation on larger grids.

The Monte Carlo DLA confirms that random-walker growth produces the same fractal structures as the diffusion approach for $\eta\!=\!1$, validating the theoretical equivalence. The sticking probability $p_s$ provides an intuitive control parameter analogous to $\eta$: low $p_s$ yields compact clusters by reducing tip screening.

The Gray-Scott model produces Turing-like patterns matching known literature results. The explicit Euler scheme with periodic BC proves stable for the chosen parameters. Different $(f,k)$ combinations produce distinct morphologies, demonstrating the rich behavior of this reaction-diffusion system.

A limitation is the absence of quantitative fractal dimension analysis. Future work could measure $D_f$ via box-counting and systematically map the Gray-Scott phase diagram.
